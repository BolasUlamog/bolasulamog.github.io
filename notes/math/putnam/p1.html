Let \(S\) be a set of real numbers which is closed under

multiplication (that is, if \(a\) and \(b\) are in \(S\), then so is \(ab\)).

Let \(T\) and \(U\) be disjoint subsets of \(S\) whose union is

\(S\). Given that the product of any three (not necessarily

distinct) elements of \(T\) is in \(T\) and that the product of

any three elements of \(U\) is in \(U\), show that at least one

of the two subsets \(T\),\(U\) is closed under multiplication.

\(\text{Essentially we have to prove that either }t_1,t_2\in{T}\implies{t_1t_2\in{T}}\text{ or }u_1,u_2\in{U}\implies{u_1u_2\in{U}}\)

\(\text{ We can attempt this using contradiction.}\)

\(\text{We can assume that there exists some }t_1,t_2\in{T}\text{ such that }t_1t_2\not\in{T}\text{ and some }u_1,u_2\in{U}\text{ such that }{u_1u_2\not\in{U}}\)

\(\text{Since }T\subset{S},U\subset{S}\text{ and }S\text{ is closed under }\times\text{, we know }t_1t_2\in{S}\text{ and }u_1u_2\in{S}\)

\(\text{Since }t_1t_2\in{S}\text{ and }t_1t_2\not\in{T},t_1t_2\in{U}\text{ since }T\text{ and }U\text{ are disjoint subsets of }S\text{ and }S=T\cup{U}\)

\(\text{Using the same logic, }u_1u_2\in{T}\)

\(\text{Using statement 5, since }t_1t_2,u_1,u_2\in{U},t_1t_2u_1u_2\in{U}\)

\(\text{However, since }t_1,t_2,u_1u_2\in{T},t_1t_2u_1u_2\in{T}\)

\(\text{Since }T\text{ and }U\text{ are disjoint this is a contradiction meaning at least one of the subsets is closed under }\times\)

\(\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\blacksquare\)
