Problem from 1995 Putnam

\(\text{Let }\)

\(1. A\subset\mathbb{R}\text{, closed under }\times\)

\(2. B,C\text{ disjoint subsets of }A\)

\(3. A=B\cup{C}\)

\(4. x,y,z\in{B}\implies{xyz}\in{B}\)

\(5. x,y,z\in{C}\implies{xyz}\in{C}\)

\(\text{Prove (At least) one of }B,C\text{ closed under }\times\)

Click to reveal the answer

\(\text{Essentially we have to prove that either }b_1,b_2\in{B}\implies{b_1b_2\in{B}}\text{ or }c_1,c_2\in{C}\implies{c_1c_2\in{C}}\)

\(\text{ We can attempt this using contradiction.}\)

\(\text{We can assume that there exists some }b_1,b_2\in{B}\text{ such that }b_1b_2\not\in{B}\text{ and some }c_1,c_2\in{C}\text{ such that }{c_1c_2\not\in{C}}\)

\(\text{Since }B\subset{A},C\subset{A}\text{ and }A\text{ is closed under }\times\text{, we know }b_1b_2\in{A}\text{ and }c_1c_2\in{A}\)

\(\text{Since }b_1b_2\in{A}\text{ and }b_1b_2\not\in{B},b_1b_2\in{C}\text{ since }B\text{ and }C\text{ are disjoint subsets of }A\text{ and }A=B\cup{C}\)

\(\text{Using the same logic, }c_1c_2\in{B}\)

\(\text{Using statement 5, since }b_1b_2,c_1,c_2\in{C},b_1b_2c_1c_2\in{C}\)

\(\text{However, since }b_1,b_2,c_1c_2\in{B},b_1b_2c_1c_2\in{B}\)

\(\text{Since }B\text{ and }C\text{ are disjoint this is a contradiction meaning at least one of the subsets is closed under }\times\)

\(\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad\blacksquare\)
