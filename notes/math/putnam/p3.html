For any square matrix \(A\), we can define \(\sin{A}\) by the

usual power series:

\[\sin{A}=\sum_{n=0}^\infty{\frac{(-1)^n}{(2n+1)!}A^{2n+1}}\]

Prove or disproof: There exists a \(2\times{2}\) matrix with

real entries such that

\[\sin{A}=\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1996 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\]

Click to reveal the answer

\(\text{We can start this proof by listing a series of known trig identities that may be useful: }\)

\(\cos{A}=\sum_{n=0}^\infty{\frac{(-1)^n}{(2n1)!}A^{2n}},\sin^2A+\cos^2A=I=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \)

\(\text{Naturally, }\sin^2{A}=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1996 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1996 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3992 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \implies \cos^2{A}=\begin{bmatrix} 0 & -3992 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\)

\(\text{However, there is no matrix }B=\sin{A}\text{ such that }B^2=\begin{bmatrix} 0 & -3992 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\)

\(\text{We can show this because let } \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} =\begin{bmatrix} a^2+bc & ab+bd \\ ac+cd & bc+d^2 \end{bmatrix} =\begin{bmatrix} 0 & \beta \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \)

\(\implies{a^2+bc=0, b(a+d)=\beta,c(a+d)=0,bc+d^2=0}\)

\(\text{Since }b(a+d)=\beta\not=0,\text{ neither }b\text{ nor }(a+d)=0\)

\(\text{This means }c=0\implies{a}=0\implies{d}=0\)

\(\text{But since }a+d\not=0,\text{ this is a contradiction so there is no }A\text{ such that }\sin{A}=\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1996 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\)

\(\text{*We can also say that }\sin{A}\text{ is a nilpotent matrix.}\)
