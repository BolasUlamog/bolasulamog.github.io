High School Graduation Speech
June 7, 2025

        The typical mundane high school experience is something clearly absent from this school’s student body. It is absent among those who pursue extracurriculars and their passions to the point they could have a linkedIn bubbling with more than the average college student. It is absent from those that spend weekends drifting golf carts and going on late night beach and hiking trips. It is absent from those that eat during class just so they can skip lunch and work on their differentials homework or scroll aops or talk politics with teachers instead of socializing. There is a level of support between the staff and students that simply doesn’t exist at other schools. This sense of camaraderie comes with a sort of transformative factor. It changes people from a tightly closed flower and allows them to blossom into a magnolia that stands out not among a field of grass but among a field of peonies and roses and hydrangeas.
        When I first went into high school I definitely wasn’t what one might call a “social butterfly”. I found myself standing still. My legs felt like they were encased in layers of thick mud while others around me frolicked around in large cliches that seemed to form out of thin air. Not that I minded playing chess or doing math or whatever else I did during lunch with the one or two friends that I had. But the actions of others forced me to turn away my disposition of solitude and recognize the importance of networking and social interaction on a variety of fronts—not just school. It was healthy to goof around every once in a while still having a strong commitment to academics and community service. It taught me that a firm tug of the wrist is all people sometimes need to become a better version of themselves.
        So I want to end this speech with a sort of prompted push towards my peers and supporters to—and this is put simply—not lose that beautiful quality of being a scintillating, guiding light for all around you. Keep supporting peers that are going through hard times. Whether it's over a failed test or a loss of a loved one or just one of those off-days. Be that roommate that they can talk to instead of the one that makes them think “I don’t wanna go back to my dorm”. Be the person that others know they can show themselves in a vulnerable state around and know they won’t be judged. But I know you can do that. Because you did it for me. Thank you.

A Short Story
February 28, 2025

        That rusty, iron taste of blood was addicting to him. He dragged his tongue across fat, chapped lips and he bit down on them, hard. Vicious cherry beads were quickly lapped up.
        To everyone else, everything was fine, but a ghastly torrent was eating him up from the inside. It was relentless, and even his own mother stood on her own island of solitary bliss. The bright sun caressed his skin until it started to burn. He stood up and walked to the shop of some stonemason of whom had moved to his village fairly recently.
        He was a grouchy old man, but Mario held him in the highest esteem. But Mario’s awkward social skills made it hard to talk to him. He ended up staring at the stonemason for a solid five minutes, then leaving without another word.
        Mario did this frequently. He was young, and told himself it was just a method for him discovering the world and learning more about himself. The old man had seemed to find annoyance in Mario’s presence at first, but later became slightly happy with it.
        Mario loved soup. Maybe instead of the taste of blood it was just that texture. Maybe he just enjoyed warm liquid falling down the sides of mouth as a result of his multitude of flaws. His mother called him to eat but it wasn’t soup today, just these chicken-egg calzone things. Their shape reminded him of the shells he used to collect when he was younger when they lived far away, closer to the beach. It made him feel this nostalgic feeling. It was hard to place.
        The food was good, as usual, but those feelings didn’t go away. He went to his room and cried silent, dry tears. Maybe as a stigma of his masculinity tears of salt were the one warm liquid he couldn’t allow to dribble down his cheeks, unless they were the result of another person’s pain. This was something he resolved to hide within himself.
        But now he felt bad because he hadn’t finished the calzone, instead storming up to his room in what he thought of to himself as an immature episode stemming from his growing depression. He walked back down but the calzone was gone. His mother must have fallen asleep.
        It was getting late, and Mario’s eyes were heavy. He asked himself why he was doing this but he had long since realized those types of questions were fruitless. But the continuous echo of the blacksmith’s hammer was gone. Mario didn’t exercise much, so his feet hurt as he increased his pace. The cold air offered an enveloping embrace to his hot starchy breath.
        His eyes must have been deceiving him though, because for the first time the stonemason looked at him it was with those same blank eyes. He didn’t know how it happened…but he knew he wouldn’t be coming back to visit the shop with the charred concrete steps and the strangely bright candy-red roof tiles for at least a while. His cries left his mind as a scream but left his mouth as all but a silent rasp. He walked down the steps and left.
