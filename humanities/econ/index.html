AP macro Notes

PPC and basic stuff (Unit 1)

Scarcity: Unlimited Wants but limited resources

Oppurtunity Cost: Most desirable alternative given up when you make a choice

Factors of production: Land, labor, capital (Machines and tools), entrepreneurship (Take the previous 3 and produce results)

Production possibilities curve (PPC): Shows the different maximum combinations of producing two different goods using all of your fixed resources

Constant oppurunity cost is linear slope, increasing oppurtunity cost is concave down

Shifts in or out with the quality/quantity of resources!

Absolute Advantage: The producer that can produce the most output or requires the least amount of resources

Comparative Advtange: The producer with the lowest oppurtunity cost

The opportunity cost of \(\text{A is }\frac{\text{cost for a unit of A}}{\text{cost for a unit of B}}\)

For output (like PPCs), the opporunity cost of \(\text{A is }\frac{\text{B}}{\text{A}}\)

Countries should specialize in the product where they can a lower oppurunity cost

Mutually benefical terms of trade: The agreed upon conditions that would benefit both countries

The terms of trade for a good will fall between the two opportunity costs!

If they trade within this range it benefits both parties

Demand Supply graphs (Unit 1)

Demand and supply:

Demand: There is an inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded

Price changes quantity demanded, not demand!

Demand shifters:

1. Tastes and preferences

2. Market Size

3. Prices of related goods (substitutes and compliments)

4. Changes in income (Normal goods: More income=buy more product, Inferior goods: More income=buy less product)

5. Expectations for the future

Supply: There is a direct relationship between price and quantity supplied

Supply shifters:

1. Input prices (prices of resources)

2. Government tools (taxes, subsidies, regulations)

3. Number of sellers

4. Technology

5. Producer Expectations

Equilibrium:

Single Shifts: Either demand goes up/down or supply goes up/down

Economic Indicators (Unit 2)

Circular flow model:

But the U.S. isn't a free market economy:

Private Sector: Part of the economy that is run by individuals and businesses

Public Sector: Part of the economy that is controlled by the government

Factor Payments: Payments for the factors of production (rent, wages, interest, profit)

Transfer Payments: The government redistributing income (social security, welfare, etc.)

Subsidies: Government payments to businesses so they can produce more

Macro Measures (GDP) (Unit 2)

3 goals for each economy: Promote economic growth, limit unemployment, keep prices stable

GDP: The dollar value of all goods and services produced within a country's borders in one year

GDP per capita is just GDP per person in that country

Percent change in gdp: \(\frac{Y2-Y1}{Y1}\cdot{100}\)

What is not counted in GDP?

1. Intermediate goods (goods used to make final goods)

2. Non-production transactions (stocks, bonds, real estate, old cars, used clothes)

3. Non-market and illegal activities (Unpaid work, black markets, etc.)

GDP = Consumer Spending + Government Spending + Investment Spending (Business spending) + NET Exports

GDP = \(C+I+G+(X-M)\) OR

GDP = Rent + Wages + Interest + Profit (This comes from adding the income made from all final goods and services produced in a year)

Nominal vs. Real GDP: Real GDP adjusts for inflation (so its the best measure)

The business cycle:

A recession is a 6 month period of decline in rGDP.

Unemployment (Unit 2)

Unemployed person: A person actively looking for a job but aren't working

Unemployment rate: The percent of people in the labor force who want a job but are not working

Labor force: The number of people aged 16+ that are willing/able to work (not in school/jail)

Labor force participation rate: The percent of population in the labor force

The 3 types of unemployment

1. Frictional: Between jobs, qualified workers with transferable skills (includes seasonal)

2. Structural: Changes to the labor force and tech make some jobs useless or skills obsolete. Workers must learn new skills

3. Cyclical: Unemployment caused from a recession (AD goes down so less labor force is needed)

Natural Rate of Unemployment: Frictional + Structural (Around 5% in the US)

Full Employment (Y): The rGDP when there is no cylical unemployment

Possible reasons for misdiagnosis of unemployment rate:

1. Discouraged workers (given up looking for a job)

2. Part time workers

Keeping prices stable (Unit 2)

Inflation: When money loses its purchasing power

Unexpected inflation helps borrowers, but hurts lenders and people on fixed income

Nominal Wages: Wages based on dollars

Real Wages: Wages based on purchasing power (adjusted for inflation)

Real interest rates: The percentage increase in purchasing power that a borrower pays. (Adjusted for inflation)

Real = nominal interest rate - expected inflation

The Consumer Price Index (CPI)

\(\text{CPI}=\frac{\text{Price of Market Basket}}{\text{Price of Market Basket in Base Year}}\cdot100\)

The base year is given an index of 100 (obviously)

GDP Deflator

\(\text{GDP Deflator} = \frac{\text{Nominal GDP}}{\text{Real GDP}}\cdot100\)

The GDP Deflator measures the prices of all goods produced. The CPI only measures prices of goods and services bought by consumers.

The 3 causes of inflation:

1. The government prints too much money

\(MV=PY=\text{Nominal GDP}\) (Quantity theory of money)

V, the velocity of money (the average amount of times a dollar is spent and respent in a year)

P, the price level

Y, the real GDP

M, the Money Supply

2. Demand Pull inflation:

Demand pulls up prices (An overheated economy with excessive spending and few goods)

3. Cost Push Inflation:

Higher Production costs increase prices

Aggregate Demand and Supply (Unit 3)

Aggregate Demand: All the goods and services (rGDP) people are willing to buy at different price levels

The Demand for everything by everyone in the USA

Why is AD downwards sloping?

1. The Wealth Effect: Higher price levels reduce the purchasing power of money. And so less expenditures happen.

2. Interest Rate Effect: When PL increases, lenders need to charge more interest to get a real return which discourages consumer spending + business investment

3. Foreign Trade Effect: When U.S. PL goes up, foreign powers buy less U.S. goods

What are the shifters?

1. Any change in \(C, I, G, \text{or } X_n\)

Aggregate Supply: Production of all firms in the economy

In the long run, the same exact quantity will be produced no matter the price level. (Full employment GDP)

Both the short and long run aggregate supply curves can shift!

SRAS Shifters:

1. Price of resources

2. Technology

3. Government regulations/taxes/subsidies

Economy at full employment!

Recessionary Gap: Actual rGDP is less than potential rGDP (the equilibrium is to the left of LRAS)

Inflationary Gap: Actual rGDP is greater than potential rGDP (the equilibrium is to the right of LRAS)

Stagflation: When the economy is stagnant (low rGDP) and inflation is high (This is a recessionary gap)

Long Run Adjustments (some examples)

1. Consumers want more stuff -> AD shifts right -> Inflationary Gap (in the short run)

In the long run, wages then go up -> cost of firms go up -> SRAS shifts left -> PL increases

2. For a recessionary gap the opposite will happen and overall PL decreases

Long run adjustment is different than economic growth!

Economic growth: The idea of rGDP going up in the long run

This can be created by an increase in investment: (What happens?)

More capital -> AD shifts right, less production costs -> SRAS shifts right, PPC moves outwards -> LRAS shifts right
