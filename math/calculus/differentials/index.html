What are differential equations?

Differential equations are equations that have derivatives in them.

Seperable Differential Equations

\(\text{Differential equations can be in several forms: }\)

\(\text{Here are some examples of }\textbf{seperable}\text{ differential equations: }\)

\(\text{Differential form: }dyf(x)g(y)=dx\)

\(\text{Derivative form: }\frac{dy}{dx}=f(x)g(y)\)

    • Introduction

    \(\text{Now we will go beyond calc I: }\)

    \(\text{Imagine a differential equation in the form }N(x,y)dy+M(x,y)dx=0\)

    \(\text{Looking back on calc III, what does this remind you of?}\)

    \(\text{Recall that the total differential }d\phi=\frac{\partial{\phi}}{\partial{y}}dy+\frac{\partial{\phi}}{\partial{x}}dx\text{. Let }\frac{\partial{\phi}}{\partial{y}}=N(x,y)\text{ and }\frac{\partial{\phi}}{\partial{x}}=M(x,y)\)

    \(\text{This means, in the case of the above differential equation, }dF=0\implies{F}\text{ is some constant. }\)

    \(\text{So, in order for }\phi\text{ to exist, }N_x(x,y)=M_y(x,y)\text{ (Clairout's)}\)

    \(\text{This means that the differential equation is }\textbf{exact}\text{.}\)

    \(\text{The solution in this case is }\phi(x,y)=C\)

    • Linear Differential Equations

    \(\text{A }\textbf{linear }\text{differential equation is a differential equation in the form: }\)

    \(a_n(x)y^{(n)}+...+a_1y'+a_0(x)y=g(x)\)

    \(\text{For now, we will focus on linear equations in the form of }a_1(x)y'+a_0(x)y=g(x)\)

    \(\text{Assuming }a_1(x)\neq0\text{, we can simplify this to }y'+\frac{a_0(x)}{a_1(x)}y=\frac{g(x)}{a_1(x)}\to{y}'+p(x)y=q(x)\)

    \(\text{And we can actually use the product rule to solve this!}\)

    \(\text{We can define some }\mu(x)\text{, where }\mu{p}=\mu'\)

    \(\text{Now (multiplying by }\mu\text{), }\mu{y}'+\mu'y=q\mu\text{, and we know from product rule that }(\mu{y})'=\mu{y}'+\mu'y=q\mu\)

    \((\mu{y})'=q\mu\implies\mu{y}=\int{q\mu}dx+C_1\implies{y}=\frac{\int{q\mu}dx+C_1}{\mu}\)

    \(\text{But what is }\mu\text{?}\)

    \(\mu{p}=\mu'\implies\frac{\mu'}{\mu}=p\implies(\ln{\mu})'=p\implies\mu=e^{\int{pdx}+C_2}=ae^{\int{pdx}}\text{, letting }C_2=a\)

    \(\text{Now we know that }y=\frac{\int{q\mu}dx+C_1}{\mu}=\frac{\int{qae^{\int{pdx}}}dx+C_1}{ae^{\int{pdx}}}=\frac{\int{qe^{\int{pdx}}}dx+C}{e^{\int{pdx}}}\text{, where }C=\frac{C_1}{a}\text{!}\)
