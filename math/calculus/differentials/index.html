What are differential equations?

Differential equations are equations that have derivatives in them.

Seperable Differential Equations

\(\text{Differential equations can be in several forms: }\)

\(\text{Here are some examples of }\textbf{seperable}\text{ differential equations: }\)

\(\text{Differential form: }dyf(x)g(y)=dx\)

\(\text{Derivative form: }\frac{dy}{dx}=f(x)g(y)\)

    • Introduction

    \(\text{Now we will go beyond calc I: }\)

    \(\text{Imagine a differential equation in the form }N(x,y)dy+M(x,y)dx=0\)

    \(\text{Looking back on calc III, what does this remind you of?}\)

    \(\text{Recall that the total differential }d\phi=\frac{\partial{\phi}}{\partial{y}}dy+\frac{\partial{\phi}}{\partial{x}}dx\text{. Let }\frac{\partial{\phi}}{\partial{y}}=N(x,y)\text{ and }\frac{\partial{\phi}}{\partial{x}}=M(x,y)\)

    \(\text{This means, in the case of the above differential equation, }dF=0\implies{F}\text{ is some constant. }\)

    \(\text{So, in order for }\phi\text{ to exist, }N_x(x,y)=M_y(x,y)\text{ (Clairout's)}\)

    \(\text{This means that the differential equation is }\textbf{exact}\text{.}\)

    \(\text{The solution in this case is }\phi(x,y)=C\)

    • Linear Differential Equations (first order)

    \(\text{A }\textbf{linear }\text{differential equation is a differential equation in the form: }\)

    \(a_n(x)y^{(n)}+...+a_1y'+a_0(x)y=g(x)\)

    \(\text{For now, we will focus on linear equations in the form of }a_1(x)y'+a_0(x)y=g(x)\)

    \(\text{Assuming }a_1(x)\neq0\text{, we can simplify this to }y'+\frac{a_0(x)}{a_1(x)}y=\frac{g(x)}{a_1(x)}\to{y}'+p(x)y=q(x)\)

    \(\text{And we can actually use the product rule to solve this!}\)

    \(\text{We can define some }\mu(x)\text{, where }\mu{p}=\mu'\)

    \(\text{Now (multiplying by }\mu\text{), }\mu{y}'+\mu'y=q\mu\text{, and we know from product rule that }(\mu{y})'=\mu{y}'+\mu'y=q\mu\)

    \((\mu{y})'=q\mu\implies\mu{y}=\int{q\mu}dx+C_1\implies{y}=\frac{\int{q\mu}dx+C_1}{\mu}\)

    \(\text{But what is }\mu\text{?}\)

    \(\mu{p}=\mu'\implies\frac{\mu'}{\mu}=p\implies(\ln{\mu})'=p\implies\mu=e^{\int{pdx}+C_2}=ae^{\int{pdx}}\text{, letting }C_2=a\)

    \(\text{Now we know that }y=\frac{\int{q\mu}dx+C_1}{\mu}=\frac{\int{qae^{\int{pdx}}}dx+C_1}{ae^{\int{pdx}}}=\frac{\int{qe^{\int{pdx}}}dx+C}{e^{\int{pdx}}}\text{, where }C=\frac{C_1}{a}\text{!}\)

    • Introduction to Higher Order Differential Equations

    \(\text{Going back to the general definition of a linear ODE: }\)

    \(a_n(x)y^{(n)}+...+a_1y'+a_0(x)y=g(x)\)

    \(\text{In order to solve an initial value problem with this }n\text{th order ODE, we will need to following initial conditions: } \)

    \(y(x_0)=b_0,y'(x_0)=b_1,...,y^{(n-1)}(x_0)=b_{n-1}\)

    \(\textbf{Existence and Uniqueness Theorem}\)

    \(\text{If the functions }a_0,a_1,...a_{n-1}\text{ are all continuous in some open interval }I\text{ containing }x_0\text{ then there exists a unique solution on }I\text{.}\)

    \(\text{So how do we solve the ODE: }p_n(x)y^{(n)}+...+p_1y'+p_0(x)y=q(x)\)

    \(\text{First, we can think of potential solutions that could work. }\)

    \(\text{Ex. Consider the homogenous linear ODE }p_n(x)y^{(n)}+...+p_1y'+p_0(x)y=0\)

    \(\text{If }y_1,y_2,...y_n\text{ are solutions to the ODE, then }y=c_1y_1+c_2y_2+...+c_ny_n\text{ is also a solution to the ODE.}\)

    \(\text{This means that the span of }y_1,y_2,...,y_n\text{ is a subset of the solution space of the ODE}\)

    \(\text{But is }y_k \text{ linearly independent? (Given the following definition):}\)

    \(y_1,y_2,...y_n\text{ are linearly independent on an interval if }c_1y_1+c_2y_2+...+c_ny_n=0\implies{c}_1=c_2=...=c_n=0\)

    \(\text{We can use something called the }\textbf{Wronskian}\text{ to find if solutions are linearly independent:}\)

    \(W(y_1, \ldots, y_n)(x) = \begin{vmatrix} y_1 & y_2 & \cdots & y_n \\ y_1' & y_2' & \cdots & y_n' \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ y_1^{(n-1)} & y_2^{(n-1)} & \cdots & y_n^{(n-1)} \\ \end{vmatrix}\)

    \(\text{If the Wronskian is non-zero, the solutions are linearly independent, meaning }y_1,y_2,...y_n\text{ span the solution set of the ODE}\)

    \(\text{This helps us to find the general solution of an ODE!}\)

    • Homogenous Linear Differential Equations with constant coefficients

    \(\text{We will now examine ODEs in the form }k_ny^{(n)}+k_{n-1}y^{(n-1)}+...+k_1y'+k_0y=0, k_i\in\mathbb{C}\)

    \(\text{First, we can assume a solution in the form }y=e^{rx}\)

    \(\text{If we plug this in...}k_nr^ne^{rx}+k_{n-1}r^{n-1}e^{rx}+...+k_1re^{rx}+k_0e^{rx}=e^{rx}(k_nr^n+k_{n-1}r^{n-1}+...+k_1r+k_0)=0\)

    \(\text{So the roots of the polynomial }k_nr^n+k_{n-1}r^{n-1}+...+k_1r+k_0=0 \text{ are the }r\text{ values in which }e^{rx}\text{ is a solution to the ODE}\)

    \(\text{The above polynomial is called the }\textbf{characteristic equation}\text{ of the ODE! }\)

    \(\text{There are 3 different cases for the roots of the characterstic equation: }\)

    \(\textbf{1. Real Distinct Roots}\text{: }y=c_1e^{r_1x}+c_2e^{r_2x}+...+c_ne^{r_nx}\)

    \(\textbf{2. Real Repeated Roots}\text{: }y=c_1e^{rx}+c_2xe^{rx}+...+c_nx^{n-1}e^{rx}\)

    \(\textbf{3. Complex Distinct Roots of form }a\pm{bi}\text{: }e^{ax}(c_1\cos{bx}+c_2\sin{bx})+... \text{Remember }e^{ix}=\cos{x}+i\sin{x}\)

    • Non-homogenous Linear Differential Equations

    \(\text{So what about the non-homogenious case? (}k_ny^{(n)}+k_{n-1}y^{(n-1)}+...+k_1y'+k_0y=g(x)\text{)?}\)

    \(\text{The general solution of this differential equation, }y\text{, is equal to the sum of the homogenous solution with the particular solution (}y=y_c+y_p\text{)}\)
