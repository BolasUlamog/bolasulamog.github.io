How do you find \(\zeta{(2)}\)?

\[ \zeta{(s)}, s\in\mathbb{C}\text{ is defined as }\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^{s}}. \] \[ \text{Let }\frac{\sin{\pi{x}}}{\pi{x}}=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n}(\pi{x})^{2n}}{(2n+1)!} \] \[ \text{This is simply the Maclaurin series expansion of }\frac{\sin{\pi{x}}}{\pi{x}}\text{.} \] \[ \text{It can be observed that } \sin(\pi{x}) \text{ can be written as a polynomial of infinite degree in the following form:} \] \[ \sin{(\pi{x})}=\pi{x}(1-x)(1+x)(2-x)(2+x)...=\pi{x}(1-x^2)(4-x^2)...=\pi{x}\prod_{k=1}^{\infty}{1-\frac{x^2}{k^2}}\] \[ \implies\frac{\sin\pi{x}}{\pi{x}}=\prod_{k=1}^{\infty}{1-\frac{x^2}{k^2}} \] \[ \text{So now we know that }1-\frac{\pi^2x^2}{6}+\frac{\pi^4x^4}{120}+...=(1-x^2)(1-\frac{x^2}{4})(1-\frac{x^2}{9})...\] \[ \text{When we expand the product expansion of sine, notice that the coefficient of }x^2\text{ is }-\sum_{n=1}{\infty}\frac{1}{n^2}\text{ because each }\frac{x^2}{n^2}\text{ must multiply with a }1. \]
