\[\text{Prove that the rational numbers are dense in the reals}\] \[\text{Let }S=(a,b)\] \[\text{We can assume } 1\lt{n}(b-a) \text{ for some }n \in \mathbb{N}\text{. (Archimedes)}\] \[\implies{b}-a>\frac{1}{n}\] \[\text{So }\{b-\frac{1}{n},a+\frac{1}{n}\}\subset{S}\] \[\text{Now consider some }p\in\mathbb{Z}\text{ in which } p\leq{n}a, n\in\mathbb{Z}\] \[\text{Consider the smallest of such }p\text{.}\] \[\text{Then it follows that }p+1\geq{na}\text{ so for some }p\text{, }p\leq{na}\leq{p+1}\] \[\text{and }\frac{p}{n}\leq{a}\leq\frac{p+1}{n}=\frac{p}{n}+\frac{1}{n}\] \[\text{Since }a+\frac{1}{n}\lt{b}\text{ and }a>\frac{p}{n}, \text{ then } \frac{p}{n}\leq{a}\leq\frac{p+1}{n}\leq{b}\] \[\text{So some rational number }\frac{p+1}{n}\in{S}\text{ }\forall\text{ closed subsets of the reals }(a,b)=S\] \[\therefore\text{ the rationals are dense in the reels}\] \[\blacksquare\]
