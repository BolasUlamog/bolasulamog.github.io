Bird

April 2026 · English 90 · Short Story

New York skylines were simply humps of gray and blue and orange at night. They weren't something that was worthy of being held in one's mind like a piece of gooey toffee sticking to the skull. The ground was coarse and angry and threw tiny pebbles at his feet, which were equally as angry but definitely more sweaty. A week ago, the sky was littered with white specks, but they quickly lost their form and blue beads now pelted his neck, as if in an act of vengeance against him and his family's only sin.

Beady eyes followed him as he walked along the concrete, some mix of dog and cat and human. He spotted others that occasionally paused to pat and stroke cinereal fur, but all he saw was a different type of gray. It was the gray of the sky and the gray of the clouds and the gray of the sidewalk and even the gray of the red bicycle that happened to pass just then.

He heard sirens and the gray became red, blue, violet and he wondered what it would be like to die. Despite his frequent yearning for death, it still felt so removed. He wanted to die with someone though. This was his selfish wish.

School started back up today. Cobbled gray and white buildings that blended into their surroundings like smeared paint across linen canvas. Hot breath surrounded him as the heap of students struggled against nipping air and the idea that not all of them could fit in the doorway at once. He sat down. He read the flyers posted on the wall about the risk of smoking and the importance of exercise. Meanwhile his teacher seemed to find it relevant to drone on about personal qualms in his life, and about the politics of allowing more than one student to go to the restroom at a time. He stood up and left. He daydreamed about becoming a superhero, and then about having the powers of a superhero but only simply that. He preferred the latter thought. But even more welcoming was the thought of not feeling the cold of snow ever again. He tasted a small amount of blood that was a result of his lack of careful application of chapstick to his lips and the dryness of New York winters. But this was enjoyable to him. The warmth was nice. The taste, albeit metallic, was nice.

He goes outside. He goes back inside. The lunch bell rings. He grabs lunch. Soup. He normally loves soup, but he knows that school lunch will be flavorless. He attempts to sit down next to her, but they all scatter like cockroaches. She left behind a notebook though. Purple sparkly unicorns across the front and so much glitter. He picked it up and opened it to the first page. No use trying to find the girl, she was long gone and had somehow migrated to the other side of the room by the time he looked around to find her. There were many doodles. Poodle dogs, smiling quokkas, fluffy red pandas, and playful sea otters. Every single page was filled similarly.

He holds it tightly as he enters class once again. Today he doesn't have to focus on the boorish posters lining the walls. He imagines what life would be like as a sea otter, and memories replay in his head of how otters hold hands when sleeping in order to avoid drifting apart when strong ocean currents arise. He closes his eyes and falls asleep into that world. He is a sea otter, and the ocean is warm and quaint to him. He sees the fish snatched up by hungry seagulls and chooses to ignore it. He is now a red panda. He swings to trees and eats bright leaves with his twelve fingers. Twelve is surely better than ten, as when he imagines himself having conversations with them or plays a game of shadow puppets on the wall it feels much less lonely. He doesn't know much about quokkas. And he hates dogs.

His mind wanders and he draws an animal, imagining what it would be like to have someone else to connect with. He considers at first drawing a sort of fluffy bird, and traces hard strokes until his lead breaks with a sudden pop. He sighs and puts his head on his arms. Enough imagining for today.

* * *

The bell rings and he leaves. He passes the collection of beady eyes, and steps through soggy snow that soaks through his shoes and socks. He opens his door, and finds once again that his mother and father are gone. He looks at the collection of clothes on the floor. None of them are his. A bright teal jumpsuit. A pair of running shorts that he knew were never worn because his mother was almost working herself to death and his dad was in a bar ninety percent of the time. The hum of the outside city and constant honking made the silence from his apartment deafening. The smell of Febreze air freshener was overpowering, but he couldn't throw it out since it was the only thing that kept his mom's sanity during the sparse times she was back home. It was cold inside since the AC was broken. Tiny bumps formed on his flesh but all he could do was pull another piece of clothing from the piles of clothes surrounding him.

His back hits the box mattress, whose cotton and coils juxtaposed against one another. It was comfy though. He pulled out the purple notebook and raised it far above his head, like a telescope raised to view stars and galaxies far away. He looked at all the drawings made by the girl. She must have been some artist. And plenty of friends. Popular. Good looking. He flipped over to his drawing, where there were but a few lines that were supposed to be the fur of a bird. He drew a short beak and eyes, but he put extra attention into making them as kind as possible. This was gonna be his pet after all. Disproportionately wide legs but at least it had a lot of fur. He never was an artist. He falls asleep with that notebook on his face and his fists tightly curled around a pencil with barely any lead.

His dreams are strangely vivid. They involve the bird pecking at his flesh until he is but a skeleton. And suddenly he is Prometheus, whose liver is eaten again and again. He convinces himself it was him. His fault and his punishment for stealing this notebook. But the pain begins to feel more and more real. He wakes up as Prometheus too. Except he is no longer bound by a chain and instead by his own fear about the nature of the world, and he is soaked from top to bottom in sweat and that bird is somehow pecking at him and the notebook is gone.

He feels guilt and shock and a mix of disgust because what is that thing? The notebook is gone. The girl will never get it back. And the bird is ugly beyond repair. He remembers his wishes of companionship under every bridge, at every coin toss where he wasted the few cents he had throwing them into a well. The bird pecks at his stomach again. It still has beady eyes, despite his attempt at making them kind and round like boba balls. It looks up at him. He imagines had it been slightly cuter he might have picked it up in his arms and held it, but the bird looked more akin to a grumpy grandma glaring at him.

It hops on his shoulder and he resigns himself. It seems quite lazy. It hops around but doesn't fly, despite having wings. He wonders if this was his fault. Maybe fur is too heavy to fly. He sighs and figures that an alive bird is better than a dead one. He wanted the last plastic container of Costco soup to himself, but he doesn't make a move to stop it as the bird frantically pecks at the open container, freshly out of the microwave. For a creature that shouldn't even exist, it ate with a ravenous, desperate energy that mirrored his own hunger. A heavy, flightless, impossible thing, sitting on his counter and making a mess of his only meal.

He snatches it back from the bird, but in a bit of a delayed reaction. The soup is mostly gone despite the bird having a malformed beak. It looks at him incredulously, and attempts to fly up to regain its food, only to fall back on the ground in a strange combination of feathers and fur.

* * *

He drinks the rest of the soup. The bird's feathers and fur were anything but gray. They were nothing like the city skyline. Despite their appearance, they smelled of bubinga and strawberry and felt like wool. If he ignored his eyes, the bird curled in his lap was just like a tabby cat that actually smelled good.

Days passed. He still felt bad about stealing the glittery book, and the bird had become more attached to him than ever. He always leaves the bird at home, and finds himself staring at the classroom posters more and more. But his pockets collect loose change and he no longer views city skylines quite as bleakly. Of course they are still quite drab and gray, but his mind wanders to the voice less than it had half a week ago. He spots the girl in the cafeteria and walks up to her. Her shoulder is soft to the touch. She looks at him with a slightly befuddled look and waits for him to say something, but for some reason his thoughts are unable to escape his mouth. His fear of being alone, of being rejected by society overwhelm him once again.

"Are you ok?" Strangely enough, the girl doesn't seem to think ill of him. He is surprised she didn't tell him to fuck off or shoot him that same candid look of disgust he gets from everyone. He tells her about the notebook. She is surprisingly not angry, but he was never the best at social cues so he doubts himself on that.

But with this a wave of serenity washes over him. He is lonely, but more so in the way that people are lonely playing video games by themselves. He returns home but the bird is gone. And he is gone now too, sitting once again in a jungle of skyscrapers where white confetti rains from the sky, soaking his boots and coat and mouth until he is no longer able to breathe. The piles that had formed on the floor become level. He is frantic. The door opens. His dad is home? But where is the bird? His dad is angry about the piles disappearing, but doesn't bother himself to discipline his kid and leaves instead. His breath smelled like a strange mix of alcohol, vomit, and coca cola.

And the bird is there, somehow camouflaged against the last standing pile, as though in some sort of war zone. It attempts to fly but instead finds itself clawing up his leg. It leaves small scratches in his flesh and a mess of rumpled hair on top of his head. But he is glad. He grabs the bird and squeezes it until it makes a sort of squeaking noise. It looks malnourished still, its feathers falling out in tufts. He feeds it more soup, but it eats very little.

He decides to bring it to school the next day. This requires a good bit of work. It refuses to be put in his backpack for a good thirty minutes, but eventually it obliges. He makes it to class late, garnering stares from classmates but he is unbothered now. The weight of his backpack comforts him. Before it was just that of a few notebooks and the sheets he turned in for school work. Lunch begins. He feeds himself and the bird. And glares at the dog that is looking at his backpack in interest, as if under the impression that he would ever let it be in control of something so precious. But the dog is no longer simply looking. It glares back and lunges at the backpack, the only thing stopping it being a clad iron chain. But he is angry. The backpack falls to the ground and he kicks the dog. Passersby and classmates stare awkwardly. The dog whimpers and backs away. But it isn't just an awkward stare. Whispers abound. Why would someone kick a dog? He hated dogs.

Memories of a childhood where his first love, a bunny by the name of Starry, was mauled by a street dog. He remembers feeling her warm blood soak through his sweatshirt and the fear and sadness that came with that. And it was always dogs. He recalls stories of classmates that abounded. Stories of dogs that ate their young and mauled puppies left in close proximity. Of pitbulls that killed children and received no further punishment than a soft reprimand because for some reason dogs were viewed as man's best friend instead of the terror that they truly were. He looks down at the bag.

But to his horror, the bird has left the bag. He scoops it up and runs away, but the people around him cast him confused looks. For all they see is the boy holding a crumpled piece of paper with a shitty drawing of a bird on it. It's back, but they don't understand that what he has is real. He has something that loves him. Do they? They have their fake friendships that are always accompanied by feelings of jealousy or lust, but do they have what he has? Never once has he felt jealousy coming from the bird now tucked back into his backpack.

* * *

He sees the kind girl. His eyes must have been deceiving him though, because for the first time the girl looked at him it was with those same blank eyes as the rest of the world. And he ran. He ran away from the world and from his source of troubles and away from everything. He ran to a world where he could be free and alone but not alone for he had his bird and that was all that mattered. He asked himself why he was doing this but he had long since realized those types of questions were fruitless. He didn't exercise much, so his feet hurt as he increased his pace. The cold air offered an enveloping embrace to his hot starchy breath. It begins to rain as if the heavens are mourning something. And he is so so exhausted. He stops beneath a bridge. There is a weird smell of sewage and damp earth, but he ignores it. Dirty water drips from the overpass onto his shoulders. He passed his home a while back, but that doesn't matter now. All that matters is escaping. Escaping from this world of torture and from his classmates and from the people that can't even look upon him kindly, as if he is some sort of alien. But they don't realize. Why can't he just be one of them?

His backpack is wet, and so is the bird when it comes clawing up his arm once again. It is cold and shivering, and he holds it and admires its beauty. Its feathers stick together from the dampness and they appear almost smeared. Its defining features blur together and he is scared. He gasps and pulls off his sweater in an attempt to dry the bird. What is happening? He fears he is Pygmalion, but without the gods at his side. What has become of him? The bird is beyond saving and he cries as the last of its features dissolve into a matte gray color. The same as the city skyline, and he chastises himself for even having the thought that the universe would choose to grace him with something that loves him. The piece of paper falls to the ground, and washes down the drain. He is gone, and so is the bird.

But somehow the notebook is there again and he realizes that he must do this at least before he kills himself.

The walk back takes much longer. He no longer has that same strength to run, and instead his feet drag on the asphalt. He gets to the school but can't find the girl. He sits on the ground with the notebook in his lap. The rain has stopped.

He hears the final bell ring. But nobody comes. He gets home just barely, and the notebook gives him enough motivation to die the next day. Now he fantasizes about killing himself in front of the girl.

He finds her in the cafeteria again. But he doesn't say anything. He sits alone. There used to be something to look forward to in those posters. But he can't even manage the energy to look at them now. He only sees the black and the red that comes with closing your eyes to the light.

He sits outside after school again. This time is the same. It doesn't rain again. He cannot be grateful for this though. The walk home is hard. The rain isn't there but it is there. He feels imaginary pellets that are so so cold hitting his scalp over and over again. He forces himself to switch to a scene of snowfall instead, but now he is even colder.

Waking up is numbing. He sees his dad hit his mom over and over again and thinks of himself as living in a cliche, except in cliches the endings are happily ever after and he knows his won't be. He sees those metal beasts racing down the streets and fights the urge to just let go and let them consume him. He is exhausted. And the same. But he notices that those drawings seemed to be a plea of desperation for her as well. And he is a ghost. They walk through him. He opens his backpack and cries when he sees nothing. His bird and his fleeting love for the world all lost to the rain.

* * *

But two days later, he feels a soft hand on his shoulder. She looks down to see pure agony. "Is that my notebook?"

The boy hands it to her, nodding in small movements that seem to be him trying to reduce his energy consumed. She grabs his hand and he offers little resistance as she navigates him through the streets of New York, as if he's a hollow skeleton with heelies. That's how he feels. He no longer feels the warmth of her hand, despite her fever. He only feels the cold of the area of his face no where his scarf has dropped from. His eyes catch a glimpse of the notebook, which is wet.

They stop in front of a restaurant in the middle of New York China Town and enter. She garbles some words he doesn't understand and now, suddenly there is a bowl of soup in front of him. And she is talking to someone on the other side. The other side of where though? And two other people with cold eyes walk in, at least he assumes, because he is unable to see their eyes in the state he's in. He doesn't eat but he wants to. He doesn't talk but he wants to. But he musters in him the energy to look and finds that the girl's eyes aren't loathsome but are kind in the way that he attempted to draw his bird to be.

And he looks at the other two. They are kind as well. And he looks at the skyline and finds that the humps of gray and blue and orange at night really are beautiful. The soup is hot and yummy.
